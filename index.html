Before anything else

What is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model, and it is the brain inside the AI tools you already hear about: ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are all LLMs. In plain words, it is a computer program that was trained on a giant pile of human writing until it got very, very good at one thing: guessing what words should come next. That guessing is why it can write back to you like a real person. Here is what it is NOT, and this matters: it is not a database, it is not Google, and it is not a person who knows things. It is a prediction machine that is shockingly good at sounding like it knows.

When you send a prompt, the LLM does not look anything up. It predicts the most likely next words, one chunk at a time, based on the patterns it picked up in all that training text. So every answer you get is really a very good guess at what a smart response would sound like, not a fact it pulled from a verified source. Hold onto that one idea, because it quietly explains almost everything else about how these tools behave.

It can be confidently wrong
Because it predicts plausible-sounding text instead of checking verified truth, an LLM will sometimes invent things, wrong dates, fake studies, made-up answers, and say them with total confidence. People call this hallucinating. That is exactly why this week you ground your agent on your own data (grounding just means it answers from the information you give it, not from guesses) and add safety rules, so it answers from what you gave it instead of making things up.
It has a training cutoff
The model only learned from text up to a certain date, so it does not actually know today's news, your clinic, or your patient unless YOU tell it in the prompt or hand it your data. When something feels out of date, it might be. You fix that by feeding it the current info it needs.
Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini are the same kind of tool
They are all LLMs, just different brands, like Toyota, Honda, and Ford are all cars. That is why this playbook lets you pick your build platform (Claude is what I recommend, with Google AI Studio or ChatGPT as backups) and why the skill carries over no matter which one you open.
It has limited memory
An LLM can only hold so much in front of it at once, a working space called the context window. Stuff too much in and older details fall off the edge. That is one reason we keep your Day 1 healthcare dataset small and focused: the agent can hold all of it at once and stay on track instead of drifting.
In healthcare this one fact is the entire game: because an LLM predicts rather than knows, it can sound dead certain and still be flat wrong, and a wrong answer about someone's health is not a typo, it is harm. That is why your agent must never diagnose, must answer only from the data you ground it on, and must refer people to a real professional. Understanding that an LLM guesses is the difference between a responsible healthcare agent and a risky one.

This LLM is the Brain of your agent, the first layer of the AI stack we break down in the next section.

What you are building

What is an AI agent?

Most people use AI as a chatbot. You open it, ask a question, get an answer, and move on. An AI agent is the step up from that. It is an AI you have set up to do a specific job, on purpose, again and again. You give it a role (who it is), knowledge (what it answers from), and rules (what it must do and must never do). Now it is not just chatting with you, it is working for you. That configured worker is your agent, and it is exactly what you build this week.

A plain chatbot
A smart stranger. It will answer almost anything, but it does not know your situation and it forgets what it is supposed to be doing the moment the chat moves on.
Your AI agent
A trained employee you briefed before their first shift. It has one job, it knows the information you handed it, and it follows your rules. Same underlying brain, a completely different result.
So tonight you are not building a general chatbot, you are building a focused healthcare agent that does ONE job (a medication reminder helper, a clinic intake guide, a benefits navigator), answers only from the data you give it, and refuses what it should not touch. That focus is the whole point. A narrow agent that does one thing well and safely beats a chatbot that does everything halfway.
Honest noteYou are going to hear this word everywhere, so here is the honest version. In the AI world, agent can also mean a fancy, fully automatic system that uses tools and takes actions on its own. You do not need that version to build something real, and you will not build it tonight. For this week, and for most real use, an agent is simply a purpose-built assistant: a model plus the setup you give it. Start there, it is more than enough.

Before the prompts

First, what is a prompt?

A prompt is just the instruction you give the AI. The AI is a tool like ChatGPT: you open it, you type what you want in plain words, you hit enter, and it writes back. That is it. If the AI is a new worker on your team, the prompt is the work order you hand it. Every single thing anyone has ever gotten out of an AI started as a prompt, so if you can text a friend, you can already do this.

Here is the part nobody tells you: the exact same AI will hand you a weak, generic answer or a sharp, useful one, and the only thing that changed is how you asked. You are not learning to code this week. You are learning to ask well, and that is the whole skill. Get good at the asking and every tool in this playbook, and every tool that comes out next year, gets better in your hands automatically.

A strong prompt usually has four simple parts. You do not have to hit all four every time, this is just a way to remember what makes an answer come back useful.
1
Role
Tell the AI who to be, so it picks the right voice and knowledge (for example, "You are a community health worker explaining things to a patient").
2
Context
Give it the background it cannot see, like who this is for and what you are working with (for example, "This is for uninsured adults in a Dallas clinic with a 6th-grade reading level").
3
Task
Say plainly what you want it to actually do, one clear ask (for example, "Write 3 questions that screen for high blood pressure risk").
4
Format
Tell it how you want the answer to look, so you can use it without cleaning it up (for example, "As a short bulleted list, no medical jargon").
Every gray box in this playbook is labeled "Starter prompt" on purpose. Those are training wheels for night one, written so a brand-new person can copy, paste, and get unstuck fast. You are NOT required to use a single one of them. The moment you can write your own, do it, because that is the actual skill this week is teaching and I want you chasing it. Change the words, swap in your own clinic or your own patient, make it sound like you. The people who win this sprint are not the ones who paste the best, they are the ones who learn to ask. If you already prompt every day, treat the boxes as scaffolding and skip straight to writing your own.

The ground rules

10 things to know about AI

You just met the LLM, the agent, and the prompt. Here is the practical version: the short list of rules that separate people who get sharp, useful work out of AI from people who get junk. Read these once. They hold up all week and long after tonight.

1
How you ask determines what you get.
Vague prompts get vague answers. Give context: who you are, what you are trying to do, who it is for, and what format you want. "Write a caption" is weak. "Write a punchy Instagram caption for a coffee brand targeting busy parents, under 15 words, casual tone" is strong.
2
It can be confidently wrong.
AI generates plausible-sounding text, which means it sometimes invents facts, fake citations, or wrong numbers. Always verify anything that matters: dates, stats, names, and any legal, medical, or financial claim.
3
Treat it like a conversation, not a vending machine.
The first answer is a draft, not the final product. Push back, ask for revisions, say "make it shorter" or "try a different angle." Iteration is where the good stuff happens.
4
Give it a role and examples.
"Act as a marketing copywriter" sharpens the output. Even better, show it an example of what good looks like. AI is great at matching a pattern you give it.
5
Ask it to think step by step on hard problems.
For math, logic, planning, or anything multi-stage, telling it to reason through the steps before it answers noticeably improves accuracy.
6
Know its limits.
Most models have a knowledge cutoff and cannot see live information unless they have web access or connected tools. It does not actually know you or remember past chats unless that is a feature you have turned on.
7
Do not paste sensitive information.
Passwords, client contracts, personal financial details, anything confidential: assume it could be stored or reviewed. Keep private data out unless you know exactly how the tool handles it. This is the same reason your agent runs on synthetic data, never a real person's info.
8
You own the output.
AI is a starting point, not the final authority. Whatever you publish or send is your responsibility to fact-check, edit, and stand behind. It speeds up your judgment, it does not replace it.
9
Use it for what it is good at.
Brainstorming, drafting, summarizing, explaining concepts, rewriting, coding help, organizing messy thoughts. It is an amplifier for tasks you already understand, and less reliable as a stand-in for expertise you do not have.
10
Experiment, and expect it to change.
The tools improve constantly. Play with different models, try the same task in two of them, and do not get too attached to one "right way." The thing you hit a wall on today may be solved next month.

MCPs
Good to know
The universal plug between AI and your stuff
In plain words
MCP (Model Context Protocol) is the USB-C for AI. Before it, every link between an AI and a tool was a custom one-off wire. MCP is one standard port so any AI can plug into your files, apps, and data without a special adapter for every pairing. Important: MCP is not an app you install, it is a shared standard, like the shape of the plug itself. The thing you actually click is called a connector, and it rides on MCP underneath.
What it lets AI do
It is how an agent reaches your real stuff: databases, Google Drive, Dropbox, Gmail, Slack, Notion, Stripe, even consumer apps like Spotify. This is what lets an AI pull in your actual data and act on your actual tools instead of being trapped in the chat window.
Where it goes
You do NOT need to build anything here, just understand WHY it matters. If you want to see it once, a free Claude user can open the Connectors Directory, click Add on something like Google Drive, and authorize it with one login (no code). To know it is real and not hype: by 2026 MCP hit around 97 million monthly downloads, and OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and Salesforce all support it. It is the new standard, so understand the concept and leave the server-building to later.
For your agent
Tonight's agent lives inside a free build tool like Claude, but MCP is the road that later lets an agent like it reach into your real healthcare tools and data.

Decide what data your agent learns from

Your agent is only as smart as what it reads. On Day 2 you are going to give it a small dataset, and it will answer questions using that data. So before you build anything, you decide where the data comes from. There are three legit sources, and you only need to pick ONE for now.

1) AI-generated synthetic data. This is the easiest one, and it is the default we use together on Day 2. You ask the AI to make up a realistic table for you. Example: 'Make a table of 15 fake clinics with name, ZIP code, services offered, whether they take Medicaid, and walk-in hours.' Nobody real is in it, you control exactly what columns you get, and it is ready in seconds. If you have never touched AI before, choose this one. You will not break anything.

2) Public or open datasets. These are real numbers the government already publishes for free, and you are allowed to use them. They are AGGREGATE, meaning they count groups of people (a county, an age band, a state), never a single named person. Real working examples for healthcare: - U.S. Census, data.census.gov, for health-insurance coverage rates by county or state (how many people are uninsured where). - CDC, data.cdc.gov, for public-health numbers like chronic disease rates, vaccination rates, and the PLACES local health data. - HealthData.gov, healthdata.gov, the U.S. Health and Human Services hub with thousands of downloadable health datasets. - Data.gov, catalog.data.gov, the giant catalog (over 500,000 datasets) where you can search a keyword like 'diabetes' or 'clinic' and filter to health. All four are real, free, and live right now. If your problem is about access or affordability, these give your agent real grounding.

3) Your own made-up examples. You type out a handful of realistic rows yourself in a note or a doc. Good when you know your topic cold (you run a clinic, you volunteer somewhere) and you just want to hand-write 10 example cases. Totally fine, just keep them invented.

Now the one rule you cannot break. Never use real personal data from a real, identifiable person. No real patient names, no real medical records, no real phone numbers, no real address tied to a real human, no screenshot of an actual person's chart, no your-cousin's-actual-diagnosis. Public aggregate stats (a county uninsured rate) are almost always fine because they are about a group, not a person. One healthcare-specific caution: glance at any row built on a very tiny population, because a single ZIP or census tract with a rare condition and only a handful of people can quietly point back to one real person even though it looks like a group number. If a public row covers thousands of people you are fine; if it covers three, skip it or zoom out to a bigger area. Data about one real human is never fine. Two reasons: it is the right thing to do in healthcare, and it protects you, because these free AI tools may train on whatever you paste in, so you never want a real person's private info going in there. When in doubt, make it up or use a public aggregate that covers a large group.

Now write it down. Add a new line to your one-page Agent Design Brief that says 'DATA SOURCE:' and name which of the three you picked plus what the data covers. Examples: 'DATA SOURCE: AI-generated, 15 fake low-cost clinics in my city with hours and Medicaid status.' Or 'DATA SOURCE: CDC PLACES public data, diabetes rates by county in Georgia.' One line. Copy your system prompt and your dataset out of the Project into a plain note (Apple Notes, Google Doc, a text file) so you own a clean backup you can rebuild from anywhere. Share the screen recording you made tonight, that is your real proof it works. Honest limit: Claude free has no true one-click 'publish' or 'export' that hands a stranger a working copy. A share link to a chat is read-only and tied to your account, it is not a product other people can run.
Make it realThe real path on Claude is the Claude API. You (or a developer friend) paste your same system prompt and dataset into a small program and Claude answers through it, which is what turns your agent into an actual website, app, or text-message bot. New API accounts get about $5 in free credits to test, then it needs a card, but it is cheap per use (Haiku, the budget model, is about $1 per million words in and $5 per million out, and a patient-facing Q&A agent uses a tiny fraction of that per question). If you do not want to touch code, a paid plan (Pro is $20/month, Team is about $25/seat/month and adds shared Projects so a clinic or class can use the same agent) keeps you in the no-code world but still does not export a standalone product. Bottom line: free Claude is the best place to BUILD and prove it, the API is the bridge to ship it.
Google AI Studio
Free, todayFree, no card: keep using your agent in AI Studio and keep your saved prompt. Click 'Get API key' to create a free Gemini API key (takes about five minutes, no credit card, free tier is about 15 questions a minute and 1,500 a day, which is plenty for a demo). 'Get code' shows the exact code that runs your agent, so you can hand the whole thing to a developer or keep it as your master copy. If you used Build mode, you can share your app so other people can open and try it in their browser, but heads up: their questions still run on YOUR free Gemini key and count against your daily limit, so it is great for showing a few people, not for releasing to the public. The genuinely free public web address is the Cloud Run Starter Tier (next section): up to 2 apps, no credit card, with tighter limits.
Make it realTwo honest routes. Route one, the free key: 'Get API key' gives you a free Gemini key and 'Get code' gives you the program, but wiring that into a finished website or app is developer work, the key alone is not a live product. Route two, 'Deploy to Cloud Run': this is the one-button publish to a public web address, and it is the most beginner-friendly real deployment in the workshop, but be careful. Cloud Run has a free monthly allowance and then it CAN charge you once you pass it, so it asks for a billing account (a card). There is a Starter Tier that lets you deploy up to 2 apps without a billing account, but it has tighter limits. Also flag for 2026: Gemini API usage is no longer covered by Google's $300 free-trial credit, so watch usage. Translation: deployable for real, low cost for a demo, but not promise-it-is-free.
ChatGPT
Free, todayFree, no card: keep using your agent inside ChatGPT and keep your saved instructions and your uploaded dataset file. You can absolutely keep refining it and showing the screen recording. Honest limit: on the free plan you can USE Custom GPTs that other people made, but you cannot create, edit, or publish your own Custom GPT, that part is locked behind a paid plan. So your free deliverable here is the working agent plus the recording, not a published link.
Make it realTwo paid doors. Door one, the Custom GPT: publishing your own GPT so others can find and use it by link needs ChatGPT Plus ($20/month). That is the no-code way to a shareable product, your dataset goes in as a 'Knowledge' file and your system prompt becomes the GPT's instructions, no coding. Note the reach limit: a published Custom GPT is usable mainly by people who are themselves inside ChatGPT, not the open web. Door two, the OpenAI API: same idea as the Claude and Gemini APIs, paste your prompt and dataset into a small program to power a real website or app, needs a card and a little developer help. Pick the Custom GPT if your audience already lives in ChatGPT, pick the API if you need it on your own site or in a clinic kiosk.
Going further, for the curious and the experienced: the single most important idea is that an API key is the bridge from a chat tool to a real product. The agent you proved on paper night one and built night two is portable text, and an API key lets that text power a website, a phone app, an SMS or WhatsApp line a patient can text, or a tablet kiosk in a clinic waiting room. That is exactly where the slash-commands, skills, MCP, and CLI ideas from the primer lead: slash-commands and skills are reusable instructions you save once and run again, MCP is how your agent safely reaches real data (an appointment system, a formulary, an eligibility lookup) instead of a static file, and the CLI is how a developer wires it all together and ships it. Be honest with yourself about the ceiling: truly free and fully no-code stops at 'it works and I can show it.' Crossing into something real people depend on usually means a paid API key (cheap per question, but it wants a card) and either a developer partner or you picking up a little code yourself. For a healthcare agent that touches real patient information, that step is also where privacy, accuracy, and who-is-accountable stop being optional, which is the whole reason this sprint treats the dataset seriously. None of that is out of reach. It is the next class, not a closed door.
